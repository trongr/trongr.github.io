Phase spaces of various systems

Example. The phase space of the motion of a system of $n$ points is described by the 3 position and 3 velocity coordinates, for a total of $6n$ dimensions. The phase space of a system of $n$ rigid bodies has dimension $12n,$ because a rigid body's states are determined by the position and velocity (6) of its center and the position and velocity (6) of any second point on the body.

Solving a first-order linear equation by integrating factors

The differential equation $$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, t)$$ is called linear if $f(x, t)$ has the form $a(t) x + b(t).$

E.g. solve $$\frac{dx}{dt} + 2tx = t$$ subject to $x = 1$ when $t=0.$

Multiply both sides by $e^{t^2}$: \begin{align*} e^{t^2} \frac{dx}{dt} + e^{t^2} 2tx &= e^{t^2} t \\ \frac{d}{dt} (e^{t^2} x) &= e^{t^2} t \\ e^{t^2} x &= \int e^{t^2} t dt \\ &= \frac{e^{t^2}}{2} + c. \\ \end{align*} Apply initial condition to find $$c = 1/2,$$ and $$x = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{e^{-t^2}}{2}.$$

Solving a first order nonlinear separable equation

Ex. Solve $$\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{x^2}{1 + t}$$ subject to $x = 1$ when $t = 0.$

A first order nonlinear equation is called separable if it has the form $$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, t) = g(x) h(t).$$ Then we can write \begin{align*} \frac{1}{g(x)} \frac{dx}{dt} &= h(t) \\ \int \frac{1}{g(x)} dx &= \int h(t) dt. \\ \end{align*} All that's left is to calculate these integrals, which in this case we can: \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{x^2} dx &= \int \frac{1}{1 + t} dt \\ x &= \frac{1}{c - \log(1 + t)}. \\ \end{align*} Apply initial conditions to get $$x = \frac{1}{1 - \log(1 + t)}.$$

Solving a second order linear homogeneous equation knowing one solution

Ex. Consider the equation $$a \ddot x + b \dot x + cx = 0,$$ where $a, b, c$ are known functions of $t.$ Suppose that we have found one solution, say $x = x_1(t),$ but have difficulty finding another. Show that by writing $x = x_1(t) u(t)$ we may reduce the equation to a first order problem for the variable $z = \dot u.$ Use this method to solve $$\ddot x - 2 \dot x + x = 0$$ subject to $x = 1, \dot x = 0$ when $t = 0.$

Suppose that the second solution $x$ of this equation may be written in the form $x = x_1(t) u(t)$ for some $u$ (it might not). Then we can find $\dot x$ and $\ddot x$: \begin{align*} \dot x &= \dot x_1 u + x_1 \dot u \\ \ddot x &= \ddot x_1 u + 2 \dot x_1 \dot u + x_1 \ddot u. \end{align*} Substituting these into the differential equation, we get \begin{align*} 0 &= a \ddot x + b \dot x + cx \\ &= a (\ddot x_1 u + 2 \dot x_1 \dot u + x_1 \ddot u) + b(\dot x_1 u + x_1 \dot u) + c x_1 u \\ &= (a \ddot x_1 + b \dot x_1 + c x_1) u + a (2 \dot x_1 \dot u + x_1 \ddot u) + b x_1 \dot u \\ &= a (2 \dot x_1 \dot u + x_1 \ddot u) + b x_1 \dot u \\ &= a x_1 \ddot u + (a 2 \dot x_1 + b x_1) \dot u \\ &= a x_1 \dot z + (a 2 \dot x_1 + b x_1) z. \\ \end{align*} Depending on what $x_1$ is, this equation may easily be solved.

Applying this method to $$\ddot x - 2 \dot x + x = 0,$$ we want to solve \begin{align*} 0 &= a x_1 \dot z + (a 2 \dot x_1 + b x_1) z \\ &= e^t \dot z + (2 e^t - 2 e^t) z \\ &= e^t \dot z. \end{align*} Therefore $z = k$ and $u(t) = kt$ for some constant $k.$ Finally, $$x = x_1(t) u(t) = k e^t t$$ is the second solution.

A common theme in solving higher order differential equations is to reduce them to lower order equations that we know how to solve. Also a common problem solving technique.

Nonhomogeneous second order linear equation

Ex. Solve $$\ddot x - x = t$$ s.t. $x = 1, \dot x = 0$ when $t = 0.$

We can solve a nonhomogeneous equation by finding (guessing) a particular solution, and adding it to the general solution of the homogeneous eq. In this case a particular solution is $$x_1 = e^t - t.$$ The general solution to the homogeneous eq is known to be $$x = C e^t + D e^{-t}.$$ Therefore the general solution to the nonhomogeneous equation is $$x = C e^t + D e^{-t} + e^t - t.$$ Applying initial conditions we find that $C = D = 0,$ so the solution is $$x = e^t - t.$$ (We could've skipped all this by noting that $x_1$ satisfied the initial conditions as soon as we found it.)

Simple harmonic motion

Let $$m \ddot x + \a x = 0$$ be the equation of motion for a linear spring with $x = x_0, \dot x = 0$ when $t = 0.$ (this can be derived using Newton and Hooke's law). Following discussion in the book we have $$\pm \int_{x_0}^{x} \frac{dx}{\sqrt{\frac{\a}{m}(x_0^2 - x^2)}} = t.$$ The integral can be found by substituting $\sin \theta = x$ to get $$x = x_0 \sin(\sqrt{\tfrac{\a}{m}} t + \tfrac{\pi}{2}).$$ Using a sum of sine formula, we get $$x = x_0 \cos \sqrt{\tfrac{\a}{m}} t.$$
